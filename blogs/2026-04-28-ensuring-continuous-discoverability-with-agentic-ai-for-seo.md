<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-understanding-the-coexistence-of-web-and-ai-agents">Understanding the coexistence of web and AI agents</h2>



<p>Before understanding agentic SEO, let’s first look at the role of AI in shaping the web. Is it staying the same, or quietly changing?</p>



<p>For a long time, the <strong>web has been more than just a collection of pages</strong>. It has functioned as an interconnected graph of entities. Websites representing people, businesses, ideas, and concepts, all linked together through content, context, and trust. This structure, often referred to as the <a href="https://yoast.com/open-web/">open web</a>, has remained relatively stable for decades. Humans created content, users discovered it through search or links, and meaning was formed through exploration.</p>



<p><em>What seems to be shifting now is not the structure itself, but how that web is accessed and consumed.</em></p>



<p>Earlier, discovery was largely a direct interaction between humans and websites. You searched, clicked, read, compared, and formed your own conclusions. Today, AI systems are increasingly stepping into that journey. They sit between the user and the web, interpreting, summarizing, and sometimes even deciding which information to surface.</p>



<p>This is where the idea of AI agents begins to emerge. Not just as tools that generate responses, but as systems that can navigate the web, retrieve information, and potentially act on it. Early examples, such as experiments in natural language interfaces like NLWeb, hint at a web that can be interacted with more conversationally, without losing its openness and interconnectedness.</p>



<p>Some refer to this shift as the beginning of an “agentic web.” But it’s important to see it less as a complete transformation and more as a layer forming on top of the existing web. The open web still exists, content is still created by people, and links still matter. What’s evolving is how that content is discovered, interpreted, and used.</p>



<p>And that shift in interaction is where things start to get interesting for SEO.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-will-seo-mean-in-agentic-web">What will SEO mean in agentic web?</h2>



<p>If AI agents are starting to reshape how people interact with the web, it naturally raises a follow-up question: where does that leave SEO?</p>



<p>For years, SEO has largely been about helping users find your content. You optimized for rankings, improved visibility on search engines, and relied on users to click, read, and navigate. But if AI agents begin to mediate that journey, not just retrieving information but interpreting and acting on it, then SEO may need to expand its role.</p>



<p>Not necessarily replace what exists, but build on top of it.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-from-ranking-pages-to-being-selected-by-systems">From ranking pages to being selected by systems</h3>



<p>In a more agent-driven environment, discoverability may no longer depend solely on where you rank, but also on whether your content is selected, trusted, and used by AI systems.</p>



<p>That introduces a subtle but important shift:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>It’s not just about being visible</li>



<li>It’s about being <strong>understandable, reliable, and usable by machines</strong></li>
</ul>



<p>AI agents don’t browse the web the way humans do. They:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Parse structured and unstructured data</li>



<li>Look for clear signals of authority and accuracy</li>



<li>Combine information from multiple sources before presenting it</li>
</ul>



<p>So instead of optimizing only for clicks, SEO may also involve optimizing for <strong>inclusion in AI-generated responses and workflows</strong>.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-stays-what-evolves-what-gets-added">What stays, what evolves, what gets added</h3>



<p>Let’s ground this a bit. Traditional SEO doesn’t disappear. Many of its fundamentals still apply, but their role may shift.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-stays-relevant">What stays relevant</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>High-quality, original content</li>



<li>Clear site structure and internal linking</li>



<li>Strong technical SEO foundations</li>



<li>Authority and trust signals (<a href="https://yoast.com/what-is-e-e-a-t/">E-E-A-T</a>)</li>
</ul>



<p>These remain essential because AI systems still rely on the web as their source of truth.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-evolves">What evolves</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Keywords → Intent modeling:</strong> Less about exact-match phrases, more about covering topics deeply and contextually</li>



<li><strong>Rankings → Presence across surfaces:</strong> Visibility may extend beyond SERPs into AI summaries, assistants, and agent outputs</li>



<li><strong>Clicks → Influence: </strong>Users may not always visit your site, but your content can still shape their decisions</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-gets-added">What gets added</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Structured, machine-readable content:</strong> Schema, clean formatting, and semantic clarity become even more important</li>



<li><strong>Content designed for extraction: </strong>Clear answers, definitions, step-by-step explanations</li>



<li><strong>Topical authority at the entity level:</strong> Being recognized as a trusted source for a subject, not just ranking for a keyword</li>



<li><strong>Freshness and adaptability: </strong><a href="https://yoast.com/tag/content-seo/">Content</a> that evolves as trends and information change</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-so-what-does-seo-really-become">So, what does SEO really become?</h3>



<p>It starts to look less like a discipline focused purely on rankings and more like one focused on continuous discoverability.</p>



<p>Or, as <a href="https://yoast.com/about-us/team/alex-moss/">Alex Moss</a> puts it in his article <a href="https://www.searchenginejournal.com/the-same-but-different-evolving-your-strategy-for-ai-driven-discovery/558619/" rel="noreferrer noopener nofollow" target="_blank">The Same But Different: Evolving Your Strategy For AI-Driven Discovery</a>, the web itself may be evolving into two parallel experiences:</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p>This has created a split from a completely open web into two – the ‘human’ web and the ‘agentic’ web… SEOs will have to consider both sides of the web and how to serve both.</p>
</blockquote>



<p>That framing makes the shift clearer.</p>



<p>Your content still needs to rank. But it also needs to work at a second layer of the web, where AI systems interpret, select, and sometimes act on information before a human ever sees it.</p>



<p>So now, your content needs to be:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Understood without ambiguity</li>



<li>Trusted enough to be referenced</li>



<li>Structured well enough to be reused</li>
</ul>



<p>In that sense, SEO doesn’t disappear in an agentic web. It stretches.</p>



<p>From helping users find information…</p>



<p>to helping systems choose it.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-role-of-agentic-ai-in-seo">Role of agentic AI in SEO</h2>



<p>If the web is gradually being experienced through both humans and AI agents, then it’s worth asking what role these agents might begin to play in SEO itself. <em>Not as a replacement for SEO teams, but as a new layer within how SEO work gets done.</em></p>



<p>What we’re starting to see is a shift from SEO as a set of periodic tasks to something more continuous, assisted, and adaptive. Some early tools already hint at this. They don’t just analyze data, they suggest actions. In some cases, they even implement changes. If this direction continues, agentic AI could become less of a tool you use and more of a system you collaborate with.</p>



<p>Let’s break down where this role might start to take shape.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-a-quick-example-structuring-content-for-machines-not-just-humans">A quick example: structuring content for machines, not just humans</h3>



<p>If agentic systems rely on structured, connected, and machine-readable content, then this isn’t entirely new territory for SEO.</p>



<p>In many ways, we’ve already been moving in this direction through structured data and schema. What’s changing is how important and foundational it may become.</p>



<p>For example, features like <a href="https://yoast.com/features/schema-aggregation/">schema aggregation</a> in Yoast SEO bring together different pieces of structured data across a site and connect them into a more unified graph. Instead of treating pages as isolated units, they help search engines better understand how entities, content types, and relationships fit together.</p>

<p>This might seem like a technical detail, but it reflects a broader shift.</p>



<p>If AI agents are parsing, combining, and interpreting content across multiple sources, then clarity and connection at the data level become more important. Not just for visibility in search results, but for how content is understood and reused.</p>



<p>So while agentic AI may feel like a new layer, some of the foundational work, like structuring content, defining entities, and building semantic relationships, is already part of modern SEO. It just becomes more critical in this context.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-so-where-does-this-leave-seo-teams">So, where does this leave SEO teams?</h3>



<p>If there’s one pattern across all of this, it’s not replacement, but redistribution.</p>



<p>Agentic AI may take on:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Repetitive tasks</li>



<li>Data-heavy analysis</li>



<li>Continuous monitoring</li>
</ul>



<p>Which leaves humans to focus more on brand-building aspects like:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Strategy and positioning</li>



<li>Editorial judgment and brand voice</li>



<li>Deciding what should be done, not just what can be done</li>
</ul>



<p>In that sense, agentic AI doesn’t redefine SEO overnight. But it does start to reshape how it’s practiced.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-understanding-the-risks-and-challenges-of-agentic-ai-for-seo">Understanding the risks and challenges of agentic AI for SEO</h2>



<p>So far, agentic AI might sound like a natural evolution of SEO. But, as with most shifts in technology, it may also come with trade-offs.</p>



<p>Not because the technology is inherently problematic, but because it introduces new dependencies, new layers of complexity, and new decisions for SEO teams to navigate. In that sense, adopting agentic AI isn’t just about adding a new capability. It may also involve rethinking how much control to delegate and where human judgment continues to play a critical role.</p>



<p>Here are some of the challenges that could emerge as this space evolves:</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-1-high-technical-and-integration-complexity">1. High technical and integration complexity</h3>



<p>Agentic systems are unlikely to operate in isolation. They may need to connect with your CMS, analytics tools, and multiple data sources.</p>



<p>This could introduce challenges such as:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Managing integrations across platforms</li>



<li>Ensuring consistent and reliable data flow</li>



<li>Defining clear workflows across systems</li>
</ul>



<p>For many teams, this might not be plug-and-play. It could require time, experimentation, and coordination across different roles.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-2-data-quality-and-dependency">2. Data quality and dependency</h3>



<p>Agentic AI may be heavily dependent on the quality of data it receives. If the data is:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Outdated</li>



<li>Incomplete</li>



<li>Poorly structured</li>
</ul>



<p>Then the outputs could reflect those gaps.</p>



<p>At scale, even small inconsistencies might influence multiple recommendations or decisions. Which is why maintaining clean, reliable data sources may become even more important in an agent-driven setup.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-3-risk-amplification-and-the-need-for-governance">3. Risk amplification and the need for governance</h3>



<p>One of the strengths of agentic AI is speed. But that same speed might also amplify unintended outcomes.</p>



<p>Without clear guardrails:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Content updates could introduce inaccuracies</li>



<li>Technical changes might lead to issues like broken links or indexing errors</li>



<li>Best practices may not always be consistently followed</li>
</ul>



<p>This is where governance frameworks and approval checkpoints may become essential, not to slow things down, but to keep them aligned.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-4-hallucinations-and-accuracy-considerations">4. Hallucinations and accuracy considerations</h3>



<p>AI systems can sometimes generate outputs that sound plausible but aren’t entirely accurate.</p>



<p>In an SEO context, this might look like:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Misinterpreted data</li>



<li>Inaccurate keyword insights</li>



<li>Fabricated or blended information</li>
</ul>



<p>The challenge is that these outputs can be difficult to spot at a glance. This suggests that validation and source-checking may remain an ongoing part of the workflow.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-5-limited-understanding-of-nuance">5. Limited understanding of nuance</h3>



<p>SEO often goes beyond data and structure. It includes tone, context, and intent. Agentic systems may not always fully capture:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Brand voice and positioning</li>



<li>Legal or compliance nuances</li>



<li>Subtle differences in user intent</li>
</ul>



<p>This could result in outputs that are technically sound, but not always contextually aligned. Human input may still play a key role here.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-6-balancing-automation-with-human-judgment">6. Balancing automation with human judgment</h3>



<p>A broader question that may arise is how much to automate.</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Too much automation might:</strong> Reduce control over strategy or brand</li>



<li><strong>Too little might:</strong> Limit efficiency and scalability</li>
</ul>



<p>Most teams may find themselves balancing the two. Using agentic AI to extend their capabilities, while still guiding direction and decision-making.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-7-high-initial-investment-and-learning-curve">7. High initial investment and learning curve</h3>



<p>While agentic systems may offer long-term efficiency, getting started could take time. This might involve:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Learning how the systems work</li>



<li>Setting up workflows and integrations</li>



<li>Aligning outputs with business goals</li>
</ul>



<p>There’s also a level of uncertainty here. The technology is still evolving, and so are the tools built around it. Which means costs, capabilities, and best practices may continue to shift.</p>



<p>For many teams, adoption may not be immediate. It could happen gradually, through testing, iteration, and figuring out what actually works in practice.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-8-zero-click-experiences-and-shifting-traffic-patterns">8. Zero-click experiences and shifting traffic patterns</h3>



<p>As AI systems become more involved in surfacing information, zero-click experiences may become more common.</p>



<p>Users might:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Get answers directly within AI interfaces</li>



<li>Interact without visiting the original source</li>
</ul>



<p>This doesn’t necessarily reduce the importance of SEO, but it may shift how success is measured. Visibility and influence could become just as relevant as traffic.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-discoverability-might-look-like-in-an-agent-driven-web">What discoverability might look like in an agent-driven web?</h2>



<p>Agentic AI may open up new possibilities for how SEO is done. But alongside that, it may also introduce new considerations.</p>



<p>It could require:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Stronger data foundations</li>



<li>Clear governance and review processes</li>



<li>A thoughtful balance between automation and human input</li>
</ul>



<p>In many ways, the goal may not be full automation. It may be a better collaboration.</p>



<p>Even if agents take on more execution, the responsibility for direction, accuracy, and trust is likely to remain human. And maybe that’s the more interesting shift here. Not whether AI agents will “take over” SEO, but how they might reshape what good SEO looks like.</p>



<p>If discoverability is no longer just about ranking, but also about being selected, interpreted, and reused by systems, then the role of SEO starts to expand. It becomes less about optimizing for a single interface and more about preparing content to exist across multiple layers of the web.</p>



<p>So the question isn’t just:</p>



<p><em>“How do we rank?”</em></p>



<p>It might slowly become:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>How to stay understandable across <a href="https://yoast.com/insights-across-multiple-llms-matter/">multiple LLMs</a>?</li>



<li>Do we remain trustworthy enough to be referenced?</li>



<li>How do we design content that works for both humans and machines?</li>
</ul>



<p>We don’t have all the answers yet. And maybe that’s okay.</p>



<p>Because this isn’t a fixed destination. It’s something that’s still taking shape.</p>



<p>And as it does, SEO may continue to evolve alongside it. Not disappearing, not being replaced, but adapting to a web that is becoming more dynamic, more layered, and a little less predictable.</p>
